OCEAN WASTE

     The first piece of research is of course the aforementioned video. In it, Tom talks about how in 1997, a cargo ship was hit with a large wave and dropped 62 containers into the sea. Some of the containers held Lego pieces, about 5,000,000 of them to be more specific. Those pieces of Lego soon started washing up on a nearby beach, and they still do to this very day. Pieces that are believed to last 1300 years before decomposition. But as the years go by, the Lego pieces (notice how i keep saying 'Lego pieces' as opposed to just 'Legos', which is apparently incorrect) are more and more scarce on the beach. Despite that, the amount of plastic that washes up there, actually grows. That is because, while the sunken Lego pieces are slowly running out, the waste produced by our society grows year by year. In the video you will hear that 80% of waste from the streets finds its way to the sea. Pretty crazy.

 

     The video gave me a good idea of the situation, and i didn't really need to know that much to make the game, but i did some more digging anyway, so here's some stats:

UNITY WEBGL

     Unity is my engine of choice for game development and a tool i'm very familiar with, so since it supports building for WebGL (Web Graphics Library), i didn't have to look for any other options. That being said, i've never tried making a browser game on Unity, and i was aware that there are certain limitations and hoops to jump through, so i had to do some more research.

     There are two places i spend the most time in: my room, and the Unity documentation site. I went to the WebGL section and started reading (the WebGL section on the docs, not in my room). Here are the articles i found most useful:

 · Getting started with WebGL development

     To my surprise, it's actually not that bad. Most of the limitations concerned things like lighting or audio, which had no bearing on the kind of game i was trying to make. But there was a bunch of things i had to keep in mind, mostly performance related back-end stuff that would take much too long to explain and has nothing to do with the assigned project anyway, i was just supposed to make an animation after all. One important thing i learned was that Unity WebGL does not support mobile devices (keep this in mind if You're going to read this entire page). All in all it looked to not be too different to regular game development. The devil lies in the details though, of course.

     After i finished reading i threw together a simple test scene with things like text, particles and other graphics.

     The scene looked fine in the preview, but the reason i made it was to see how it'll look in the browser. To do so i had to 'build' the project. That's where i hit the first hurdle.

     Unity was created as an engine for desktop, console and mobile games, i.e. stored locally and ran via their own executable. The fact that you can make web games is just a bonus. This results in Unity having to recompile all of the game code and dependencies into something that the browser knows how to deal with. On top of that, the game has to be severely compressed, so that it doesn't take too long to load off of the internet. These two processes, plus the usual compilation time, add up to seven entire minutes of build time on my lethargic computer. This doesn't sound like much of an issue until You realize that every time i change or add something, i have to wait 7 excruciating minutes to even see if it works right in the browser, sometimes even more.

THE FISH

     Same way as all humanity came from a fish, the development of this game too begins with a fish.

     I booted up Illustrator and quickly made a simple fish sprite.

     The reason it's white is so that by changing the hue while maxing out the saturation within the engine, i can make the fish whatever color i want (you can do it in the game too with the scrollwheel but it's a secret so shhh).

     Now it's time for the movement.

     At first it might seem simple: just set the position of the fish to the position of the cursor on every frame. But with that approach, there is going to be a problem with collision detection further down the line. The fish gets hit by an obstacle whenever its position falls inside the area of the obstacle within a frame. But if the player moves fast enough, there might not be any frame where that actually happens, leaving the fish unscathed. This isn't acceptable, as the player can just wiggle their mouse around and evade most of the waste, even when moving directly through it.

      In order to combat this, we're going to use the same technique that i use in my actual game, which is something that i call a 'tween collider'. In simple terms, it stretches the 'hittable' area of the fish from the current position, to the position the fish was in last frame. With the collider visible (the green streak), it looks something like this:

     Now we are almost done with the movement. Emphasis on 'almost'. Right now we are just changing the position of the fish, but to make it feel like actual movement we have to actually make it FEEL like movement, using animation. There are three elements that make up the fishes' animation:

     The flailing sells the illusion of the fish actually moving in relation to the water and the waste despite being in the same place in relation to the screen. It also makes the fish feel much more 'alive'.

     The flailing speeds up depending on how fast the player is moving horizontally in order to make the motion feel more realistic and the fish more 'reactive' with the player's inputs.

     The fish tilts towards the vertical direction of the player's movement to make it feel more loose and relaxed as opposed to stiff and mechanical. It is done by calculating the angle from the fish to the player's cursor.

     There remain two issues:

     Issue #1: Depending on how often the obstacles get spawned, either the game barrages you with waste, making the game too daunting, or spawns too little of it, making the game too boring. To address that, the game starts of with just a few bottles and bags, but slowly increases the amount of waste created, making for a nice difficulty curve.

     Issue #2: When a piece of waste is successfully evaded and goes off-screen, it keeps moving to the left infinitely, clogging up precious memory. To avoid that, waste gets automatically destroyed when reaching a certain distance off-screen. When a piece of waste is destroy, i can also simply add 1 to the score count, catching two fish with one net.

GAME CYCLE

     Now it's time for the boring stuff. Whenever the fish gets hit with a piece of plastic the game stops counting the score and a game over window pops up. When the player hits 'retry' all of the waste gets destroyed and the difficulty system resets back to it's default. It may sound simple but took ages to code. The one fun thing about this entire segment is the death animation and effects. This is made up of 3 main parts:

1) The fish gets flung upwards, then descends off-screen. This is done by giving the fish some vertical velocity and then reducing it linearly.

 

2) The screen shakes, making for some nice impact. This is done by changing the camera height with a sine wave.

 

3) The fish turns gray and its eye gets crossed out.

MOBILE VERSION

     In the Unity documentation, it says clear as day that

     So while making the game, i didn't even take into consideration how it would look or work on phones. Making a multi-platform game takes much more time, and there is a lot more things to consider during development, so i was honestly glad that i didn't have to worry about anything like that. It should also be noted, that it takes even more work and time to convert a pre-existing non-multi-platform game, like mine, into a working multi-platform game. You probably already see where i'm going with this.

 

     So i uploaded the "'finished"' game onto the web, to see how it works on my laptops browser. It worked fine. But out of curiosity, i also checked what shows up when you try to open the page on mobile. A blank screen? An error message? The full game working completely fine despite what the documentation was saying but not at all designed to work on mobile devices??? Ding ding ding, we're in trouble!

     Now, the obvious thing to do at this point is to just block the game from running on phones. Saves a ton of work, and the game wasn't supposed to run on mobiles anyway, so nothing's changed.

 

     So i spent the entire day redesigning the game to work on phones, because i guess i just hate myself. Going through every single line of code, changing almost everything to make sure it presents well on vertical screens and with touch controls, it was exhausting. It may not seem like much, but i left out a lot of things while writing this page, as most of it is things like recalculating the obstacle spawn and kill plane positions when resizing the window, or making sure that the fish doesn't get hit by a piece of waste from a previous playthrough while restarting the game. Boring over-complicated programming things that really don't require an explanation here. And i had to change all of it. The one and only way i managed to make the job easier was by simply rotating the camera -90°, which saved me some hassle when it comes to making the game work vertically.

     Kids, don't trust anything you read on the internet, especially the Unity documentation.
